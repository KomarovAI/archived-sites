Why Indoor Humidity Matters

Controlling the humidity levels inside your home is more than just a matter of comfort. High humidity levels create an ideal environment for mold and mildew growth, triggering allergy symptoms, worsening asthma, and damaging your property. Over time, it can also cause wood to crack, shrink, or warp, damaging furniture, instruments, and other belongings. 

Knowing the signs, potential causes, and steps to take when your AC is causing humidity problems helps you catch the issue early on to prevent health issues and structural damage.

How Does an AC Control Humidity?

Air conditioners are a helpful tool in controlling humidity levels within your home. Throughout the cooling process, they remove moisture from the air to maintain balanced levels.

The role of your AC in moisture removal

Several AC components contribute to the moisture removal process. When warm air passes over the evaporator coils, the coils cool the air, causing the moisture in it to condense into water droplets. These droplets then collect on the coils, where they’re drained away through the condensate line. After removing the moisture, your AC circulates cool, dehumidified air back into your home.

Common AC humidity problems

When something goes wrong with your air conditioner, it can prevent the system from dehumidifying the air. The most common air conditioner humidity issues include:

  • Dirty evaporator coils
  • Clogged drain lines
  • Low refrigerant levels
  • Incorrect thermostat settings
  • An oversized system
  • Leaking ductwork
  • Poor ventilation
  • Lack of maintenance

If your Carrollton home is feeling hot and sticky, there are a few culprits that could be the cause. Failing components, poor maintenance, or improper installation can all keep your air conditioner from effectively removing moisture from the air.

Signs your AC isn’t removing humidity

Recognizing the signs that your AC is struggling to maintain humidity levels allows you to catch the issue early, preventing it from escalating into costly repairs. If you notice any of the following, schedule an AC inspection with Medley to restore your home comfort:

    • Clammy or sticky air: Damp or heavy-feeling air inside your home is one of the top signs that your air conditioner is allowing humidity levels to rise.
    • Musty smells: If you notice a musty odor around your home, it may be due to excess moisture that’s allowing mold and mildew to grow.
    • Condensation: Visible condensation on your windows, walls, or other surfaces is a clear indication that your AC is not effectively pulling moisture from the air.
    • Inconsistent temperatures: If your AC is struggling to dehumidify the air, some rooms may feel warmer than others due to the excess moisture in the air.
  • Wall damage: Peeling paint or bubbling wallpaper is often caused by high humidity levels that trap moisture behind surfaces.

DIY Tips To Reduce Indoor Humidity

There are a few easy ways you can keep indoor humidity levels balanced. Optimizing your air conditioner, installing additional systems, and promptly addressing AC repairs help reduce moisture levels, keeping your home cool and comfortable throughout the hot summer months.

What homeowners can check

The best way to control humidity levels is to check that your air conditioner is set to the proper settings and functioning correctly. Here are a few tips to help your AC perform at its best:

  • Set your system to “dry mode” or “economy mode,” which are specifically designed to dehumidify the air.
  • Set the thermostat to “auto” instead of “on” to prevent the fan from recirculating moisture.
  • Schedule AC maintenance for humidity issues at least once a year to keep all components functioning properly.
  • Turn your AC fan speed to a lower setting to help improve humidity removal.

Along with optimizing your air conditioner, there are some other strategies you can use to keep humidity levels down, including:

  • Installing a dehumidifier in areas with excessive humidity.
  • Turning on exhaust fans in your bathrooms and kitchens after cooking or showering.
  • Checking for proper ventilation throughout your home.
  • Addressing leaks as soon as possible to prevent moisture from spreading.

What’s the Difference Between a Gas and Electric Water Heater?

Gas and electric water heaters warm water for showers, laundry, and dishes, but they differ in operation. Their designs, installation requirements, efficiency levels, and other characteristics vary in ways that affect energy bills, convenience, and safety.

  • Gas water heaters: These units heat water with a burner at the bottom of the tank, fueled by natural gas or propane. They require venting to release combustion gases and a gas line connection for fuel.
  • Electric water heaters: These systems use electric resistance coils to heat water. They’re easier to install since they don’t involve combustion or venting and only need a dedicated 240-volt electrical circuit.

Pros and Cons of Gas vs. Electric Water Heaters

Both types of water heaters offer advantages depending on your goals and your home’s infrastructure. Consider performance, efficiency, and ownership costs to weigh the pros and cons of gas and electric water heaters.

Upfront cost

Electric water heaters tend to cost less upfront since installation is simpler compared to gas units. Gas water heaters usually have higher initial installation costs to account for the added complexity of adding exhaust systems and gas line connections.

Energy efficiency and long-term operating costs

Since they convert almost all electrical energy into heat, electric models are generally more energy efficient than gas water heaters. However, electricity rates may be more expensive in certain areas, which can result in higher bills.

Gas heaters have slightly lower efficiency ratings due to heat loss through venting. Even so, gas is often more affordable than electricity, which may save more on utility bills over the heater’s lifespan, particularly if your household has high hot water demand.

Speed & hot water recovery time

Gas water heaters typically heat water more quickly than electric units, and this recovery time makes a difference for busy households with heavy usage habits. Electric heaters recover hot water more slowly, which may cause delays during peak usage.

Safety, installation, and maintenance

Electric water heaters don’t use ventilation or combustion, so they don’t carry risks of carbon monoxide or natural gas leaks. Maintenance is also simpler since electric units have fewer and less complex components than gas models.

Gas heaters have more complex installation and safety requirements. Maintenance includes periodic burner, flue, and ignition system inspections to minimize the risk of gas-related hazards.

Lifespan & reliability

Gas and electric water heaters last about 10 years, though electric units can maintain reliable and efficient operation for up to 15 years. Electric models are more resistant to internal corrosion because they operate at lower temperatures and don’t involve combustion.

How Do I Know Which Water Heater Is Right for My Home?

Deciding which is better between gas or electric water heaters depends on your home’s utility connections and your household’s needs. Here are a few questions to help guide your decision.

  • Do you have access to natural gas or propane? If your home already has a gas line, a gas water heater may be more economical — otherwise, electric models are often the simplest and most affordable option.
  • Is your household small, average, or large? Large households may prefer the recovery speed of a gas unit.
  • Do you want the lowest upfront cost or long-term savings? Electric units are generally less expensive to install, but gas models may offer better long-term value.
  • Is safety or installation speed more important to you? Electric heaters are easier to install and don’t carry the risk of gas or carbon monoxide leaks. Gas heaters are safe with proper installation, but require more planning and labor.

Mini-Split vs. Central Air: Which One Is Right for You?

When your cooling system is due for an upgrade, it can be challenging to determine whether a mini-split or central air system is better for your home. Central air conditioners offer convenient whole-home cooling but require a duct system to keep cool air flowing throughout your space. On the other hand, mini-splits offer energy-efficient, customizable cooling at a higher upfront cost. In this guide, we’ll explore the pros and cons of ductless mini splits vs. central AC systems to help you make the right choice for your home.

What’s the Difference Between a Mini-Split and Central Air?

While mini-splits and central air systems both help keep homes cool, they differ in how they distribute cool air throughout the space. 

  • Central air conditioning: Uses an indoor air handler and an outdoor condenser. It draws warm air in via ductwork, cools it with refrigerant, and recirculates it with ducts and fans.
  • Ductless mini-splits: No ductwork needed. Each zone has an indoor unit that cools and recirculates air directly into the space.

What Are the Pros and Cons of Mini-Splits vs. Central Air?

Central air conditioners and ductless ACs offer unique benefits and drawbacks, making them better suited for unique situations. Understanding the pros and cons of each system helps you make the right choice for your home. 

Energy efficiency comparison

When cooled air travels through your ductwork, it can escape through leaks and cracks, preventing it from reaching your living spaces and forcing your central air system to use more energy. Mini-splits avoid this energy loss by eliminating the need for ductwork and delivering cool air directly into your space.

Installation considerations

Mini-splits offer more convenient installation in homes without existing ductwork, as they don’t require extensive construction or air duct installation. Central air systems, on the other hand, can be more complex and time-consuming to install but offer convenient whole-home cooling for spaces with an existing duct system.

Comfort & control

Mini-splits allow you to control each indoor unit individually, allowing you to customize temperatures in certain areas of your home. Central air conditioners provide consistent, whole-home cooling, eliminating hot spots and temperature variations between rooms. 

Aesthetics and noise

Central air conditioners are often concealed in attics, basements, or utility closets, keeping the equipment out of sight and reducing noise levels in your space. Mini-splits are mounted on the walls, making them more visible and potentially distracting.

Common Scenarios: Which System Works Best Where?

The right cooling system for your space depends on your layout, cooling needs, and budget. 

Mini-splits work best for:

  • Garages, add-ons, and sunrooms
  • Older homes without ductwork
  • Individual rooms or multi-zone control
  • Energy-conscious households

Central air works best for:

  • Newer or existing ducted homes
  • Larger homes needing even cooling
  • Families wanting a hidden system
  • Homeowners planning whole-home HVAC upgrades

How Do I Know Which System Is Right for My Space?

Before choosing a cooling system, ask yourself the following questions: 

  • How large is the space you’re trying to cool? Central ACs are best for large homes that require even cooling, while mini-splits are ideal for small areas or individual rooms.
  • Do you already have ductwork installed? Homes without existing ductwork often benefit from the straightforward, duct-free installation of a mini-split.
  • Are energy savings or upfront costs more important? While central air systems may have lower upfront costs, mini-splits offer long-term energy savings for years to come.
  • Do you need both heating and cooling? Unlike central air systems that only produce cool air, mini-splits offer two-in-one heating and cooling capabilities.

AC Suddenly Stops Working? A Clogged Drain Line Could Be the Culprit

Nothing is quite as frustrating as an air conditioner that won’t turn on when you need it. As your local AC repair pros, condensate drain line repair is one of the most common jobs we’re hired to do. A simple fix to this component could be what you need to get your air conditioner up and running again.

What Is a Condensate Drain Line—and Why Does It Matter?

Your condensate drain line is responsible for redirecting the condensed moisture that is accumulated during the air cooling process. This helps to prevent water from building up in the system and overflowing, helping you avoid water damage to your home and to the components of your air conditioner.

Issues with the drain line can easily shut down your air conditioner. A safety feature called a float switch will engage if too much water enters the system, triggering a system shutdown to prevent electrical hazards and water damage. To get the system running again, you’ll need to address the air conditioner water leak at the source, which typically means fixing the drain line.

Signs Your Condensate Drain Line Is Clogged

Let’s look at the top clogged AC drain symptoms for homeowners to lookout for:

  • Water pooling around the indoor unit.
  • AC turning on and off frequently (short-cycling).
  • Moldy or musty odors near vents.
  • Wet air filter or drain pan overflow.
  • AC stops running with no obvious reason,

These signs can indicate an issue with the drain line or pan, but the only way to know for sure is to consult a professional HVAC technician.

A technician will confirm this common AC problem in a few ways, such as with a visual inspection, testing of the float switch, and line flushing. Using these methods, they’ll determine if the drain line is the source of the issue, which will help them determine the next steps to get the system running again.

Can You Unclog the Drain Line Yourself?

If your AC keeps shutting off and you suspect the drain line is to blame, there are a few ways you can try to fix the issue yourself.

Simple DIY methods

AC not draining water? Try fixing the issue yourself with these easy DIY methods:

  • Flush the drain line using vinegar and/or hot water.
  • Use a wet/dry vacuum from the outdoor line to remove the clog.
  • If accessible, clear the line from the access point using a drain snake.

When to call a professional

If your AC is not working and you can’t seem to fix it yourself, it’s time to call a professional. The same goes for drain lines with multiple clogs, as it can be very difficult to remove them all yourself. If there is any possibility of electrical risk or water damage, or if your float switch continuously triggers after being reset, we strongly recommend contacting a professional. Attempting to fix this issue yourself can lead to serious injury from electric shock or extensive water damage to your home.

Risks of Ignoring a Clogged Drain Line

There are several risks of inaction if you notice a drain line issue during your springtime AC maintenance. Pooling and leaking water can lead to water damage to surrounding floors and drywall, which can potentially lead to mold growth in and around your HVAC system. This can also put excess strain on AC components, causing damage to the blower motor or circuit board and increasing future repair costs.

No one likes being forced to pay for AC repairs. But by addressing drain line issues now, you’ll be paying a small amount compared to the costs of replacing the unit and getting water remediation after HVAC failure.

What Does the Fan Motor Do in Your AC System?

The fan motor moves air through your HVAC system and into the ductwork, helping regulate the temperature in your home. When your AC fan is not working, your air conditioner can’t distribute air, leading to uneven temperatures, poor airflow, and an uncomfortably hot house.

Understanding the role of the fan motor

The fan motor drives air across the evaporator coils and into your ductwork. It powers the blower fan to distribute this cool air through the duct and into your home. 

Types of fan motors

Cooling systems contain two fan motors, which operate the blower and condenser. The blower motor operates inside your indoor unit to push cool air through your ductwork, while the condenser motor works outside to expel heat from the system.

These motors come in single-speed (on or off)  or variable-speed operations that change speed based on your cooling needs to provide more precise temperature control.

Signs Your AC Fan Motor Might Be the Problem

With your fan motor responsible for distributing air throughout your cooling system, it’s not surprising that it’s one of the most common culprits when airflow issues arise. Knowing the warning signs your air conditioner fan is not working helps you catch problems sooner, so you don’t have to deal with more costly repairs down the line.

Common warning signs

Some of the top indicators of fan motor troubles include:

  • No air coming from the vents when the AC is on.
  • Your AC unit is making humming noises.
  • The outdoor unit is running, but the indoor air handler is staying silent.
  • Overheating smells or short cycling.

How to tell it’s not something else

The fan motor isn’t always to blame when your AC stops blowing air. Check for the following to rule out other common problems:

  • A dirty or clogged air filter
  • Tripped circuit breakers
  • Malfunctioning thermostat settings
  • Blocked or closed vents

Can You Fix an AC Fan Motor Yourself?

When it comes to fan motor repairs, it’s best to leave the job to the professionals. These components are wired into your system’s electrical network and often require specialized tools for removal and replacement. If you’re unsure whether the fan motor is the issue, you can perform a few tests to rule out other problems.

Simple checks you can do

Before calling in the pros, try these troubleshooting tips to see if you can get airflow back up and running:

  • Inspect for tripped breakers
  • Check the thermostat for error codes or incorrect settings
  • Carefully try spinning the AC fan blades to see if they activate
  • Clean the air filter to rule out clogs or blockages

AC Fan Motor Repair or Replacement: What to Expect

Build-up inside pipes

Leaks from corrosion

A pipe leak is one of the biggest culprits when it comes to rusty pipes and water pressure drops. Rust eats away at the side of a pipe until the water can escape, lowering the pressure in your faucet or shower head.

Blocked fixtures

Due to flaking rust, your fixtures can become blocked, reducing the amount of water that can pass through. Over time, this problem noticeably affects your water pressure.

Water pressure symptoms

Common Causes of a Furnace That Keeps Turning On and Off

While the underlying cause of a furnace that is short cycling varies, there are a few common reasons why this might occur.

Dirty or clogged air filters

A dirty or clogged air filter restricts airflow in your furnace and makes the system work harder and overheat. As a result, the unit may turn itself off as a safety measure to prevent system malfunction, resulting in short cycling. 

Once your system cools off, the system turns on again, and the cycle repeats. Understanding the cause of your furnace woes is crucial to keep your system running smoothly. Fortunately, fixing clogged air filters in furnaces is simple – just replace the dirty, clogged filter with a new one. 

Faulty thermostat

A faulty thermostat can cause short cycling. Here are some of the main reasons why your thermostat might have issues:

  • The thermostat is not set to “heat” mode.
  • Your thermostat could have faulty or damaged wiring.
  • A thermostat that is improperly calibrated will turn on and off until it’s fixed.
  • Digital thermostats with low or “dead” batteries will often malfunction.
  • Thermostats placed near a heat source like a sunny window could give false readings and malfunction.

Oversized furnace

An oversized furnace will heat your home too quickly, causing the thermostat to turn the unit off. Once the temperature drops, it turns on again and repeats the cycle until you correct the issue. Heat exchanger or limit switch problems

A cracked or damaged heat exchanger can cause gases to leak and cause the unit to overheat and turn off. Dirt or debris clogging the heat exchanger will also lead to overheating, causing the limit switch to turn the unit off when it senses high temperatures.
